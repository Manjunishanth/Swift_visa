## 1. Overview â€” What â€œSchengenâ€ means

* The **Schengen Area** is a group of European countries that have abolished systematic internal border controls for travelers moving between them.
* A **Schengen short-stay visa (Type C)** permits stays in the Schengen Area for **up to 90 days in any 180-day period** for tourism, business, family visits, short studies, or transit.
* **National (D) visas** are issued by individual member states for longer stays (work, study, family reunification, long-term residence). Rules for D visas are decided by each member state.

## 2. Key Universal Rules (Apply across Schengen)

### 2.1 The 90/180 Rule (Short-stay limit)

* A non-EU/EEA national may stay in the Schengen Area for **a maximum of 90 days within any rolling 180-day period**.
* This is cumulative across all Schengen states (not per country). The 180-day window is rolling (count backwards from each day of intended stay).

### 2.2 Visa Types (Short summary)

* **Type C (Schengen short-stay):** tourism, business, family visit, short studies up to 90/180.
* **Type D (National):** long-stay (>90 days) for study, work, family reunification, residence. Issued by individual member states; may allow limited travel within Schengen for short periods.
* **Airport Transit Visa (Type A):** transit through international transit zones at airports (rare; many nationals exempt).

### 2.3 Biometric & Application Basics

* Applicants normally **must provide biometric data** (fingerprints + photo) at the visa application center (VFS/consulate), once per 59 months (approx.) unless exempt.
* Schengen visas require a **personal application** (rare exceptions for children or specific categories).

### 2.4 Travel Medical Insurance (Mandatory for short-stay)

* For Schengen Type C visas, travel medical insurance is **mandatory** and must cover **medical emergency and repatriation**.
* **Minimum cover**: **â‚¬30,000** (this is the commonly required minimum across Schengen consulates). Insurance must be valid for the entire period of intended stay and across the whole Schengen area.

### 2.5 Purpose & Supporting Documents (Short-stay)

Typical required documents (vary by consulate / nationality):

* Valid passport (valid for at least 3 months beyond intended departure from Schengen; with at least two blank pages).
* Completed and signed visa application form.
* Recent passport-style photos.
* Proof of purpose: invitation letter, hotel bookings, conference confirmation, enrolment for short course.
* Proof of financial means to cover stay.
* Proof of travel medical insurance.
* Return/onward travel tickets or reservation.
* Proof of accommodation.
* Visa fee (varies).
* For minors: birth certificate, parental consent, custody proof.

## 3. Financial Requirements / Proof of Means

* **No single EU-wide fixed bank balance** is mandated; consulates evaluate whether the applicant has sufficient means for the duration and purpose of visit.
* Acceptable evidence includes: recent bank statements, salary slips, sponsorship letter (formal obligation by host), confirmed paid hotel reservation, prepaid tours, or proof of financial sponsorship.
* Typical practice: consulates look for bank statements covering the **last 3 months** and at least modest, proportional funds relative to the trip length and destination cost-of-living.
* If invited/sponsored, a **formal obligation** or invitation letter combined with sponsorâ€™s bank proof may replace applicant funds.

## 4. Visitor / Tourist Visa (Type C) â€” Practical Rules

* Purpose: tourism, visiting friends/family, business meetings, cultural/sports events, short study (<90 days).
* **Proof of ties to home country** is important: employment letter, property, family, enrolment.
* **Intent to return** is assessed; lack of return ties increases refusal risk.
* **Travel insurance** and itinerary/reservations are required.
* **Invitation letters** should include host details, relationship, duration and accommodation commitment.

## 5. Short-Term Study & Training

* For courses **<90 days**: short-stay (Type C) is appropriate. Provide course confirmation, tuition payment proof, accommodation.
* For degree programs or courses **>90 days** â†’ apply for national long-stay (D) student visa at the specific country consulate.

## 6. Work & Long-Stay (National D Visas & Residence Permits)

* **Employment authorizations are country-specific.** Each Schengen state has its own work permit and national visa rules (e.g., Blue Card, skilled worker permit, seasonal worker permit).
* Typical pattern:

  * Employer obtains work permit or labor clearance (varies by state).
  * Applicant applies for **national D visa** for entry/initial stay.
  * After arrival, applicant applies for a residence permit with local authorities.
* **Salary & labor market tests** are decided by the issuing member state; your bot must consult the country-specific document for numeric thresholds or occupation lists.

## 8. Family Reunification & Dependent Visas

* Family reunification rules differ by state but commonly cover:

  * Spouses/registered partners
  * Minor children
  * Dependent adult children or dependent parents (in some cases)
* Sponsorâ€™s obligations: proof of accommodation, income sufficiency, relation proof, sometimes integration orientation / language proof.

## 9. Health & Vaccination Rules

* No general EU-wide vaccination requirements for entry; however some member states may request medical checks for long-term residence.
* Travel insurance with medical evacuation is mandatory for short-stay visa.
